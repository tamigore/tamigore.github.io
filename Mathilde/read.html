Rue Ronde

I often dream of this place called Rue Ronde.

Bubble shop

1.My best friend, Marine, owns a bubble shop next to the main square - or circle, rather. Her bubbles are strong, big and made by a small box fan that blows through a circle of strings dipped into soapy water at regular intervals. You can have a plain bubble if you are like. I never have a plain bubble. I ask Marine to fill mine up with the trembling voice of Barbara. Unfortunately for my cousin Nils, who I brought to the store one summer day last night, the bubbles aren’t thick enough to contain the high-pitched notes of Die Zauberflote. He left the store disappointed, in his silent bubble, which we shared, for they are very resistant to laughter, these bubbles.

Art gallery

2.This art gallery is located next to a vintage underwear store. You have to enter it through a neighboring street that runs parallel to rue Ronde. I didn’t know I would end up on rue Ronde at all as my mother and I wandered through Brooklyn and happened upon the place. The open wooden door led to a rather steep slope covered in blue and pink balloons floating near the ground all the way to a large opening on the other side. The balloons did not slide down the slope or spill onto the rue. There were lamps on the walls made from slightly flattened pink and blue balloons arranged into bouquets of two or three. We liked those. My mom and I detached one of the lamps from the wall to take it home, while the artist, dressed in a dark blue tailcoat and top hat, talked to someone wearing yellow overalls and a big orange afro just outside the shop. Once we got the lamp, we started carefully descending the slope, holding the lamp tight against our stomachs so the artists wouldn’t see it. As we enjoyed the strange sensation of the balloons under our feet, we felt the artist rushing behind us and started hurrying ourselves. Tripping on the balloons, we hurtled down the slope, rolling on the ground. We reached the bottom of the slope, deliciously dizzy. As I pulled myself up from the balloons, I put on my most candid smile and saw the artist’s frown disappear as her eyes met mine. She sighed resignedly and climbed back up the slope without a word. The lamps had popped in our fall anyways. As my mom and I walked away on rue Ronde, I noticed animated postcards on the walls of angry bears screaming “It’s mine!” and “This is mine!”.

Twin churches

3.The church has an identical twin. In front of the two churches, an old lady sells flowers from one of the book stands they usually sell books from by the Seine. As I walked by, a man blasted a song I liked from his car, so I danced on the sidewalk. The old lady joined in and the way she danced was very funny. She sang jokes in a rocky voice, which she ended by ringing the bell she held in her left hand with the stick in her right. I decided to buy a bouquet of big yellow flowers and smaller pink flowers from her. I didn’t want them to be too old, that way they would have time to bloom, but I ended up with a bouquet of buds. She took me to the anarchist theater held by her son and his wife. It’s right around the corner. We went up a flight of stairs and found an adorable family of four sitting on two couches facing each other. I looked at flyers for the anarchist plays. The youngest boy looked at me with his big grey eyes. He said he recognized me from that time I brought a red rose to a guy I liked at the theater. I pretended I didn’t know what he was talking about.

Saloon

4.At number 7, there is a huge saloon where real cactuses grow from the hardwood floor. All the guys there have mustaches, even the guy I used to love who is playing folk music on our old couch between two spiky plants. His folk features singers who sit on his lap. His guitar student Jimmy is singing now. Jimmy was born on a mountain and has never seen the sea. He’s a little bit of a mama’s boy, and he mumbles melodies about his mama and the sea. The sweet sea. He doesn’t know it’s salty. I tell him and he doesn’t believe me. His mama smiles. She wears a blue bikini. She is excited for him to discover the sea and the things beyond it, even though it sounds a little scary to Jimmy. They talk about floating on their backs to other countries. He doesn’t know it’s far. He imagines the sea to be a sweet still pool with floating fishies. I tell him about the waves and he looks at me like I'm pulling his leg. What kind of organism could to and fro like that tirelessly?

Subway

5.There is a subway with three stops underneath rue Ronde. Two of the stations are painted blue, but the third one is orange. The counter to buy your ticket is on the wrong side of the turnstiles. I can see ticket inspectors in their blue outfits standing by the platform, lazily checking passengers’ tickets. I wait there for a while, not knowing what to do, worried they might notice me hesitating. Finally, I crawl under the turnstiles and stand in line to get my ticket. By the time I get the ticket, the inspectors are gone, so I don’t bother going back out and through the turnstiles, I just get on the train with my unused ticket.

Uncle's office

6.My uncle’s office is an open space where the cubicles are made of public library shelves that store mostly children's books. He is a writer. Everybody is busy, talking on the phone, searching the Internet. My dad, mom, and I are visiting him and he is proud to show us his desk and his old grey computer. The patio outside however is very gloomy. There is one dry tree growing from a whole in the concrete, and some trash cans. There are a couple of lonely looking graffitis. And rats. Big, fast rats on pigeon legs. One of the rats starts running after me, it wants to bite into my big toe. I try to kill it with a trash can lid, but I am too slow and he corners me against a wall. Nobody knows what to do, until a tall figure wearing a dark hood runs towards me and kills the rat with a scythe made of pieces of public library shelves, and then disappears.

Sewer

7.One night when I had a cold, I dreamt of the sewer. It was filled with snot.

Boat pier

8.I’m not sure how Sofiane, Emre and I got from Place de la Concorde to the rivière Ronde. The river runs in a circle and you never know how long it will take you to get back around to where you started. It was morning and we were still pretty tipsy. We were lucky to get there when we did because there was a small boat moored to the stone quay, pulsating with morning house music, ready to leave. A small Staline-looking man held a dirty string open with his pudgy fingers, to let us through. He handed us yellow tickets and said something grumpy in Turkish. We stood there, confused, even though Emre speaks Turkish, but the man was already looking away, so we stepped onto the boat, where a few high people were dancing. We danced for a while as the boat made its tranquil way through a bright green pine forest, and the sun rose until 11 a.m. The Staline-looking Turkish man put on an apron and served us stacks of pancakes covered in a gooey pink sauce on cardboard plates. He sat down with us in a plastic chair around a plastic table and said more grumpy things about being tired and wanting to go home. We offered him some pancakes but he didn’t want any.

River branches out

9.There is a place where the river branches out into two streams and one becomes shallow, almost a brook, and passes over the other, like a river highway exit. I was standing in the higher stream and my friends were standing in the stream below. The water barely reached my chins. We were fishing for a rare octopus, called the Tiger Octopus, with big landing nets. They had done this before but it was my first time. I was very focused, looking carefully for movement amongst the rocks, when I felt something slimy between my toes and saw two grey eyes peeping at me from between my feet. I immediately placed the net in the octopus’ way and watched it get caught slowly. We were all very excited. It was beautiful, too, the colorful dots on its back and tentacles dancing in the net. I was allowed to keep one octopus, but I set it free anyway.

Ikea

10.There is also an IKEA. You have to wait in line through the night to get in. They give you blue anti-contamination suits and plastic covers for your shoes which you put on in a locker room before entering. Then you have to grab and drag a string dangling from a single rail in the ceiling. The strings are dirty and have a red sprinkler head attached to the end of them, which makes them hard to hold on to, but I got one of the older ones from which the sprinkler had fallen off. You have to run and jump through all the different rooms. Sometimes, when you run, you get pulled forward by the motion of the string you were dragging behind you a second ago. In one of the rooms, there was a black chalkboard on which a Russian girl and I drew a big orange tree. She thought the tree was so pretty that she kissed me. Then we had to run out because the IKEA was closing.

Loft

11.I lived in a loft with a river that ran through two of the rooms, but mostly mine. It ran like a rapid in the far corner of my bedroom and served as a trashcan and a jacuzzi. We built a dam with white tree branches in my roommate Joe’s room so the trash wouldn’t pollute the rest of the stream, but Joe wouldn’t clean it. He didn’t seem to mind the empty cans and wrappers stuck to his naked butt when he sat in and felt the pressure of the cool water. The water started slowly rising and pouring out of the garage next door.

Basement

12.In the basement lives a Native American man. He wears a black Akubra hat and a long black coat. His black hair is braided in a single thick and shiny braid that falls down his back all the way to his knees. He is friendly. I stayed in a room in his apartment one night. The apartment was minimalistic and bright. There was a simple wooden cross on the wall facing my bed. I woke up at sunrise and stepped out onto the cool white tile of the kitchen to get some water. I saw him sitting on his bed through the open door to his room. I invited him into his kitchen. He didn’t want any water. We looked good in the morning light. We watched through the long, horizontal window above the sink as a little girl ran by. She was waving a long, pastel ribbon that flew behind her. He told me she was coming back from a rave on top of a rock.
